Frequently asked questions

Attending

Who can attend?

The event is open to all policy professionals working across central government. This includes those in departments, devolved administrations, arm's-length bodies (such as agencies, regulators, and non-departmental public bodies) and local government.

It is also for people working in policy roles in local government.

We're also keen to see people in policy adjacent roles whether that's in central or local government, academia, think tanks or civil society.

By bringing together people working on 'policy' at different levels and in different contexts, the event will create space for richer discussion, deeper insight sharing and more meaningful connections.

Why should I attend?

As a policy professional in government — this is your opportunity to step away from your desk and engage with peers across government and the policy making landscape. You will have a chance to take part in open, practical discussions shaped by issues that matter most right now – leaving with ideas, insights and approaches you can apply directly in your role.

As a policy professional in local government — this is a chance to share your on-the-ground experience and learn from others working in different places and contexts. You'll explore what works in practice, exchange ideas with peers across the system, and gain new perspectives to support delivery and local impact.

As someone who works in academia, civil society or a think tank — this offers a unique opportunity to engage directly with policy practitioners. You'll have the chance to share your experiences of policy influencing and gain an understanding of the realities of policymaking in practice, while building connections across sectors.

I want to attend but need to convince my seniors. What should I tell them?

You can explain that Policy Camp is a valuable opportunity to:

  • Learn how others are tackling similar challenges across government, local government and beyond
  • Reflect on and improve your own approach to policy development and delivery
  • Build connections that can support future collaboration

We're also happy to provide further details to support internal approval if needed.

The day

What can I expect on the day?

The event is an unconference. After an initial welcome, attendees are invited to come forward with a 'pitch' about a topic. This will often be an idea, concept or question you want to discuss with others.

Pitching is relatively informal – you will have thirty seconds to present your topic. There is no need to prepare anything in advance or to have slides ready. There will be an option for attendees to nominate someone to pitch a session on their behalf.

After pitching, a brief show of hands is taken to consider space and room sizes, and the topic is added to a session grid, which is built in real time. In this way, attendees set the agenda for the day and can choose how to spend their time most valuably.

If your topic is selected, you won't be expected to present on the topic. Instead, you'll act as a convener, helping to frame the conversation and drawing on the experience of others in the room.

Sessions are discussion-based and collaborative. People are encouraged to move between sessions (often called the "law of mobility"), so discussions stay energetic and ideas can spread.

Do I have to pitch a session if I attend?

No. You're welcome to attend Policy Camp and not pitch a session.

Many participants prefer to join discussions, share perspectives, and learn from others. The focus is on open conversation and collective learning — there's no expectation that everyone contributes in the same way.

If my session is added to the grid, what should I expect?

Try to get to your allocated room a few minutes before your session starts.

Think of yourself as a chair of a roundtable, rather than a speaker. Your role is to put forward the topic, ask questions and invite others in for their insights. The value of the session will come from open discussion and collective problem solving.

How will you handle sensitive discussions?

We encourage participants to prioritise discussing topics they are comfortable sharing openly, so that everyone can get the most out of the day. However, we also recognise that the value of an unconference is in attendees bringing forward the issues that matter most to them, and this may include sensitive topics.

To that end, the event will operate under Chatham House Rule. This means discussions will be anonymous, and while participants can discuss insights from the sessions, they will not, and should not be, attributed to specific individuals or organisations.

Session conveners will also have the option to run their sessions without note-taking and/or to run closed sessions for a specific audience.

Our Code of Conduct sets out expectations to ensure a respectful and inclusive environment for all participants.

Practical information

What should I bring on the day?

Please bring photo ID for registration. You must have a ticket in your name to enter the event. You don't need to print your ticket — just bring photo ID (a government pass is fine).

Where can I find an agenda for the day?

Once built, the agenda will be hosted on a physical board at the event.

Do I need to bring a laptop?

We try to keep conversations digital-free. However, you are welcome to bring one if you'd like.

Are there quiet spaces available?

Yes, we have a quiet room and a prayer room.

Do I have to stay for the whole day?

While we'd love it if you stayed for the whole day to get the full experience, you don't have to. The beauty of an unconference is that the format is designed to be flexible, and you're encouraged to shape the day around what's most useful to you. If you can only attend part of the day, you're still very welcome.
